<section class="study-section" id="ch-01">
  <p class="chapter-kicker">Chapter 1</p>
  <h2>Project+ Foundations and Certification Overview</h2>

  <h3>Project Basics</h3>
  <p>A project is temporary work undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result. Temporary means the project has a beginning and an end; it does not necessarily mean short or simple. Projects have a reason or purpose and are different from ongoing operations.</p>

  <h3>Projects vs Operations</h3>
  <p>Operations are ongoing business activities that can continue indefinitely. A project may create or change something, then hand the completed output over to operations. A useful exam distinction: projects are temporary and unique, while operations are ongoing and repeatable.</p>

  <h3>Lifecycle Order</h3>
  <p>The CompTIA Project+ lifecycle order is Discovery / Concept Preparation, Initiation, Planning, Execution, and Closing. Discovery clarifies the business need or opportunity. Initiation formally authorizes the project. Planning defines how the work will be managed. Execution performs the work. Closing finalizes acceptance, releases resources, archives records, and captures lessons learned.</p>

  <h3>Programs and Portfolios</h3>
  <p>A program is a group of related projects managed together, usually because their outcomes, dependencies, resources, or benefits are connected. Program management focuses on coordinating those interdependencies. A portfolio is a collection of projects, programs, and operations managed together to support organizational objectives. Portfolio management is about selecting, prioritizing, balancing, and governing work so it aligns with strategy.</p>

  <h3>Certification Overview Notes</h3>
  <p>The Project+ exam is aimed at IT professionals who need to manage small projects. The exam may include a maximum of 90 questions.</p>

  <p class="study-note">Test-day cue: project equals temporary plus unique plus purposeful. Operations equals ongoing.</p>
</section>

<section class="study-section" id="ch-02">
  <p class="chapter-kicker">Chapter 2</p>
  <h2>Comparing Project Management Methodologies</h2>

  <h3>Waterfall / Predictive</h3>
  <p>Waterfall is a predictive, sequential approach. Work moves through defined phases and is usually planned heavily upfront. It fits best when requirements are stable, major changes are not expected, the work is predictable, and stakeholders are comfortable with formal updates at longer intervals. Construction-style work is a common example of a project type that may fit predictive delivery.</p>

  <h3>Agile / Adaptive</h3>
  <p>Agile is an adaptive approach based on iterative and incremental software development. It is useful when requirements may change, when feedback is important, or when value needs to be delivered early and often. Agile emphasizes collaboration, customer feedback, frequent delivery, and continuous improvement.</p>

  <h3>Agile Manifesto Values</h3>
  <p>The Agile Manifesto values individuals and interactions over processes and tools, working software over comprehensive documentation, customer collaboration over contract negotiation, and responding to change over following a plan. The items on the right still matter, but Agile places more value on the items on the left.</p>

  <h3>Agile Principles</h3>
  <p>Agile principles prioritize customer satisfaction through early and continuous delivery of valuable software. They support welcoming change, frequent delivery, close collaboration between business and technical people, motivated teams, face-to-face communication, working software as a progress measure, sustainable pace, technical excellence, simplicity, self-organizing teams, and regular reflection.</p>

  <h3>Selecting an Approach</h3>
  <p>The right approach depends on environmental factors, company culture, organizational structure, industry expectations, stability, uncertainty, schedule and budget constraints, funding availability, and stakeholder expectations. Day-to-day stakeholder involvement and uncertain requirements often point toward Agile. Stable requirements, predictable work, and occasional formal stakeholder updates often point toward Waterfall.</p>

  <h3>Hybrid Approaches</h3>
  <p>A hybrid approach combines predictive and adaptive practices. It can be useful when some parts of the project are stable and can be planned upfront, while other parts are uncertain and benefit from iteration.</p>

  <p class="study-note">Test-day cue: stable and predictable tends to Waterfall. Changing, uncertain, feedback-heavy, or early-value work tends to Agile.</p>
</section>
