  <h2>Project Business Case</h2>
  <p>The project business case is the value proposition for a proposed project. It explains why the project is worth considering and may include both financial and non-financial benefits.</p>

  <h2>Project Selection Methods</h2>
  <p>Project selection methods help compare possible projects before work is formally approved. They support discovery and concept planning by giving decision-makers a way to evaluate value, cost, constraints, and expected benefits.</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Linear programming:</strong> a method used for constrained optimization.</li>
    <li><strong>Internal Rate of Return:</strong> shows whether an investment has a high or low rate of return. Bigger is better.</li>
    <li><strong>Benefit-Cost Ratio:</strong> compares the benefits gained with the costs incurred. It answers how much money comes back for each dollar invested.</li>
    <li><strong>Net Present Value:</strong> based on the assumption that money is worth more now than in the future.</li>
    <li><strong>Payback Period:</strong> shows how long it will take to pay back what was invested in the project.</li>
  </ul>

  <p class="study-note">Exam cue: higher IRR is better, BCR should be greater than 1, and shorter payback is usually better.</p>

  <h2>Scoring Model</h2>
  <p>A scoring model is an objective project selection method. The team identifies relevant criteria, weights them according to importance, and then scores each project against those criteria. Each score is multiplied by the criterion weight, and the project with the stronger total score is usually the better candidate.</p>
